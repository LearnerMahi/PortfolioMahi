# My Competitive Programming Journey at KUET

When I started my CS degree at KUET in 2022, I had a solid GPA from school but barely knew what a segment tree was. Two years later, I've solved 132 problems on Codeforces and 72 on LeetCode, and helped my team place **9th at the Intra-KUET Programming Contest 2024**.

Here's what that journey looked like.

## The First 3 Months — Pure Confusion

The first time I opened a Codeforces problem, I thought: "This is just math with code." I was wrong. It's a different way of thinking entirely.

My mistakes in the beginning:
- Writing brute-force solutions for everything
- Not thinking about edge cases
- Copying solutions before truly understanding them

The breakthrough came when I stopped caring about rating and started focusing on **actually understanding** each problem after solving (or failing) it.

## The Algorithm Turning Points

A few topics that completely changed how I think:

**Two Pointers & Sliding Window** — Once you *see* the pattern, you see it everywhere. Problems that looked like O(n²) suddenly become O(n).

**Binary Search on the Answer** — Not just for sorted arrays. Searching on a monotonic function is one of the most elegant techniques in CP.

**Graph Theory** — BFS/DFS are the foundation. Once you add Dijkstra, Floyd-Warshall, and topological sort to your toolkit, a whole class of problems opens up.

## Lessons I'd Tell My Freshman Self

1. **Solve problems slightly above your level** — struggle is the learning mechanism
2. **Read editorials only after giving it 30+ minutes** — not immediately when you're stuck
3. **Implement from scratch** — don't rely on copying snippets you don't understand
4. **Teamwork in ICPC style is a different skill** — communication and problem distribution matter

## What's Next

I'm working toward Codeforces Division 2 performance and preparing for inter-university contests. The combination of competitive programming and my thesis work in graph analysis has been surprisingly synergistic — algorithm thinking applies everywhere.

If you're just starting out: consistency beats intensity. 30 minutes a day beats a 6-hour weekend session every two weeks.
